CARBOXYLIC ACIDS (–COOH) Quick Summary
Definition

Carboxylic acids contain the carboxyl group (-COOH).

General Formula:

R–COOH

Examples:

HCOOH → Formic acid (Methanoic acid)
CH3COOH → Acetic acid (Ethanoic acid)
C6H5COOH → Benzoic acid
Structure of Carboxyl Group

The carboxyl group contains:

Carbonyl group (C=O)
Hydroxyl group (–OH)

The carbon is sp² hybridized and planar.

Physical Properties
Boiling Point

Highest among similar molecular weight compounds because of hydrogen bonding.

Order

Carboxylic acid > Alcohol > Aldehyde > Alkane

Solubility

Lower members are completely soluble in water.

Higher members become insoluble due to long alkyl chain.

Acidic Strength

General Order

Carboxylic acid >> Phenol >> Alcohol

Reason:
Resonance stabilization of carboxylate ion.

Effect of Substituents

Electron withdrawing groups increase acidity.

Example

CF₃COOH > CCl₃COOH > CH₂ClCOOH > CH₃COOH

Electron donating groups decrease acidity.

High-Yield NEET Facts
Carboxyl carbon is sp² hybridized.
Carboxylic acids form dimers through hydrogen bonding.
Formic acid is the only common carboxylic acid that reduces Tollens' and Fehling's reagents.
LiAlH₄ reduces carboxylic acids to primary alcohols.
NaHCO₃ distinguishes carboxylic acids from phenols by releasing CO₂.
Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity; electron-donating groups decrease it.
HVZ reaction requires an α-hydrogen and uses Br₂/red phosphorus.
Decarboxylation with soda lime removes one carbon atom from the chain.
Grignard reagents react with CO₂ followed by acidification to form carboxylic acids.
Carboxylic acids have the highest boiling points among comparable organic compounds due to strong intermolecular hydrogen bonding.
